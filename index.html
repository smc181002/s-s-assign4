Introduction to Signals
What are Signals and Systems?
0:00 - 10:00 Time Stamp for the video about what signals and systems are
  • This is a very intuitive video introducing us to the concept of signals and systems. It stresses the fact that signals and systems are intertwined in our daily lives.
  • Signals and systems are of no use to us individually. We generally use systems to alter the signals in a certain manner – like changing the form of a signal (Ex.: electrical to mechanical), scaling its amplitude, or even modulating it – so that they are of use to us.
  • In real life, a system works in a very complicated manner, with many signals being operated upon; with the possibility of subsystems being integrated into a larger system.
  • However, the simplest form of a system can be understood as an entity which manipulates one or more signal (inputs) to form a certain function, thereby yielding new signals (output).
  • A signal, on the other hand, is a mathematical function that conveys information about some phenomenon. They may be a function of time or any other quantity, and they may be continuous or discrete. But all types of signals are important in real life and the study of S&S.
What are MEMS? (Applications)
0:00 - 8:59 Time Stamp for the video about MEMS
  • MEMS is quite an important application of Signals and Systems. Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems are a category of devices that are microscopic in nature and mainly constitute electrical sub-systems with moving parts.
  • The video captures the basic information about MEMS. They are produced using a process known as microfabrication. Depending on their functionality, they may gather information about the surroundings (sensors) or may follow certain commands (actuators).
  • In general, they are mainly used for perception and control in modern devices. In this regard, the electrical components process the data collected, while the mechanical parts act on the processed data.
  • The advantage of MEMS is that they use lesser components than any ordinary electrical system, which increases accuracy and lowers the cost. They find applications in automobile safety systems, video projectors, smartphones and even pedometers.
SONARS (Applications)
0:24 - 1:16 Time Stamp for the video about SONARs
  • SONAR is short for Sound Navigation and Ranging. It is usually used in water bodies and uses high-intensity sound waves.
  • Active SONAR harnesses the property of reflection of sound, and the time is taken for the echo to reach the receiver of the SONAR system, to locate the seabed or a foreign object (enemy submarines, shipwrecks etc.).
  • Passive SONAR, on the other hand, emits no signals, but detect acoustic signals from them, informally known as “listening”
RADARS (Applications)
0:05 - 1:01 and 2:58 - 5:19 Time Stamps for the video about RADARs
  • RADAR is short for Radio Detection and Ranging.
  • In principle, it is similar to SONAR. However, it uses electromagnetic waves (specifically, radio waves) to detect objects, even in the cosmos. It uses the reflection property in this regard.
  • A more improved version of this device is the Doppler RADAR, which uses the Doppler effect to estimate the speed of a moving object based on the sound it emits.
Analog and Digital Signals
What are analog and digital signals?
1:48 - 4:06 Time Stamp for the video about analog and digital signal
  • A signal whose amplitude can take any value in the continuous range is called analog signal. The continuous condition is only for the amplitude but the signal can be discrete in the time axis.
  • In the case of Digital signals, the amplitude can only take fixed finite values on the amplitude axis
  • Since the condition of continuity is only on the amplitude axis, the signal can be continuous or discrete and still be either analog or digital depending on the amplitude values.
Sampling of Signal (Analog to Digital)
1:10 - 5:35 Time Stamp for the video about Sampling and sampling theorem
  • Sampling is a way to convert the continuous signal to a discrete signal because of a system cant store infinitely large values.
  • In this process, we store enough values to closely represent the actual signal
  • The number of sample points to choose in a time interval is not arbitrary to make the signal conversion lossless
  • Nyquist Shannon sampling Theorem states that any continuous time signals can be converted to and from digital signals without any loss in the time axis when they are sampled at a rate at least twice the highest frequency of the signal
Quantization of Signal (Analog to Digital)
0:16 - 6:36 Time Stamp for the video about Sampling and sampling theorem
  • Now after completing the sampling process, according to the digital signal definition, the amplitude cannot be continuous.
  • So we convert the continuous amplitude into finite levels to convert it into digital signal
  • The process of quantization unlike sampling is not lossless.
  • The loss in this process is dependent on the number of levels we provide. The lesser the loss, more close the transformed signal is to the original one
